What is a McMansion?

'Money is the McMansion in Sarasota that starts falling apart after 10 years' - Frank Underwood

Part I : McMansion Defined

McMansion is a spin-off of the term Millenium Mansion which was coined by Virginia Savage McAlester.
McMansions are large - usually overly-large - mass-produced, extravagant homes that lack architectural integrity. There definition of a McMansion is slightly ambiguous, as there is no one defintion. However, McMansions have a distinct look to them while failing to adhere to many common design principles.

Part II : Common Properties McMansion

Every house is different.... except for a McMansion.
McMansions are not just ugly houses, they satisfy some, if not all, of the following properties

  • Large: obvious and in the name, McMansions are usually > 2500 sqft
  • Many Styles, No Cohesion: McMansions attempt to imitate existing architecture. This can be a single source of inspiration, or with multiple buildings that don't work well together. Either way, it fails.
  • No Architectural Principles: this is explored in the next section, there are many characteristics of well-designed buildings and McMansions fail to implement them.
  • Cheaply Built: while McMansions appear oppulent, they often times use cheap materials (vinyl siding, manufactured stone veneer)

Part III : Architectural Integrity

Mass

Mass refers to the shape and cohesion of the different exteriors of the building. The primary mass is the largest shape of the building. Secondary masses are other exterior elements that form the fa√ßade. Entries (windows, doors, etc) are referred to as voids and act as seperators between the masses.

Balance

Balance is the cohesiveness of different parts of the building on both sides of the centerline. Balanced buildings are not always symetrical, though the 'weight' or masses on each side are generally equal in presence. McMansions fail to implement any kind of balance.

Harmony

Harmony refers to the consistency of different elements of the building. Buildings should have a balance of voids and masses. Elements themselves should remain consistent and proportional with one another.
